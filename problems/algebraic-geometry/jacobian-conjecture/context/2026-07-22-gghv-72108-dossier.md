## 1. Identification of the open case

[VERIFIED] [GGHV22] Abstract (p.1): "We list all the pairs (deg(P), deg(Q)) with
max{deg(P), deg(Q)} < 125 for any hypothetical counterexample to the plane Jacobian Conjecture and
discard them all, except the pair (72, 108) (and the symmetric pair (108, 72)), thus we confirm the
lower bound of 100 obtained by Moh and raise it up to 108."

[VERIFIED] Intro (p.1, tex lines 251-252): "The only exception is the case (deg P, deg Q) = (72, 108),
and so, if one manages to discard this case, it would increase the lower bound from 108 up to 125."

[VERIFIED] Intro (p.2, tex lines 255-268): assuming deg(P) < deg(Q), the 5 remaining cases satisfy
(deg(P), deg(Q)) in {(56,84), (66,99), (72,108), (80,120)}, "where there are two cases with
(deg(P), deg(Q)) = (72,108)". Section 5 of the paper discards (66,99) and ONE of the two (72,108)
cases (the one with A0 = (9,27)). Exact quote: "For the other case with (deg(P), deg(Q)) = (72, 108)
we couldn't solve the corresponding system of polynomial equations, thus it is left open."

[VERIFIED] Theorem 2.1 (p.2): "If (P,Q) is a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture, then we have
either max{deg(P), deg(Q)} >= 125, or (deg(P), deg(Q)) in {(72,108), (108,72)}."

The rows shown in red in the paper (marked here by their citations) are the 5 considered already solved.
Of the remaining five, the paper discards (7,21) and (8,32) in its section 3, (9,24) and (9,27) in its
sections 4-5 ((7,21) again in section 6). The row "(8,28) *(3,2)" is the only survivor.
[UNVERIFIED] The asterisk "*" in "*(3,2)" is not explained anywhere in the source (tex line 314 reads
`$(8,28)$&*(3,2)& 108 & -\\`); the natural reading is that it flags the case left open, but the paper
never says so.

Degree bookkeeping [DERIVED, trivial]: v_{1,1}(A0) = 8 + 28 = 36; with (m,n) = (3,2) the pair has
degrees {3*36, 2*36} = {108, 72}. So in the paper's deg(P) < deg(Q) normalization of the intro this case
is (deg P, deg Q) = (72,108); in the table's (m,n) = (3,2) labeling it is (108,72). After the reduction
of Proposition 4.3 (below) P carries the factor 2 and Q the factor 3.

[VERIFIED] Closing statement of the intro (p.2, tex lines 273-277): "However, the Jacobian conjecture
is a very hard problem, so we have to combine these techniques in order to be able to increase the lower
bound of 100 for max{deg(P), deg(Q)} established in 1983 by Moh in [10], up to 108. With enough
computing power we would be able to raise it up from 108 to 125, since there is only one case left."

## 2. The reduced Newton polygon for the open case: Proposition 4.3 (pp.10-12)

This is the paper's complete structural statement for the open case. There is NO analogous "system of
polynomial equations" section for (8,28) in the paper (see section 4 of this dossier); Proposition 4.3
is where the paper's treatment of the open case ends.

### 2.1 Statement [VERIFIED, verbatim from tex lines 1000-1007]

Proposition 4.3 (Case (8,28)). If there is a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture in the case
(8,28), then there exist P, Q in L^(1) with [P,Q] = x^2 and one of the following cases holds:

1. N(P) = {(0,0), (1,0), (8,14), (8,16), (0,8)},
   N(Q) = {(0,0), (2,1), (12,21), (12,24), (0,12)}.
2. N(P) = {(0,0), (1,0), (8,14), (8,16)},
   N(Q) = {(0,0), (2,1), (12,21), (12,24)}.

Note: L^(1) = K[x, x^{-1}, y] and the commutator is x^2, NOT x. Both points matter for any replication:
the reduction uses a non-polynomial automorphism of L^(1), and the (8,28) case is the only one of the
four reduced cases whose normalized commutator is x^2 (the (9,27), (9,24) and (7,21) reductions all
end with [P,Q] = x).

### 2.2 Proof pipeline, step by step [VERIFIED, close paraphrase of tex lines 1008-1311]

Step 0 (initial shape). "We first prove that the corners of the polygons of P and Q in this case are
given by {(0,0), (1,0), (8,28), (0,4)}, multiplied by (m,n) = (3,2)." So the hypothetical pair starts as
N(P) = 3*{(0,0),(1,0),(8,28),(0,4)} (deg 108) and N(Q) = 2*{...} (deg 72); A0 = (8,28), A0' = (1,0)
(the figure on p.10 labels A0 = (8,28) and A0' = (1,0)).

Step 1 (swap). Apply the automorphism phi_1 with phi_1(x) = y, phi_1(y) = x. Use [1, Corollary 7.4]
with (rho_0, sigma_0) = (-1,4), l = 1 and (a/l, b) = (28,8). Then en_{-1,4}(F) = (21,6) = (3/4)(28,8),
"which means that q = 4 in the corollary, and for (rho,sigma) = Pred_P(-1,4) we have
(rho,sigma) in ](0,-1),(1,-1)] and there exists a (rho,sigma)-homogeneous element R, such that
l_{rho,sigma}(P) = lambda R^{qm} = lambda R^{4m} and v_{rho,sigma}(R) > 0. Thus en_{rho,sigma}(R) = (7,2),
and there exists a (rho,sigma)-homogeneous element G satisfying [R,G] = lambda R^i for some i.
Therefore, we are in the setting of [2, Proposition 3.12]. By [6, Proposition 2.5], necessarily
Pred_P(1,0) in {(1,-2),(1,-3)}." (Figure caption p.10: "Pred_P(1,0) in {(1,-2),(1,-3)}"; the swapped
polygon has vertices (28,8) with lower-edge candidate endpoints (4,0) and (12,0).)

Step 2 (cut the lower edge; three branches). [VERIFIED, tex lines 1073-1090]
- "By the same proposition, if Pred_P(1,0) = (1,-2), then l_{1,-2}(P) = lambda (z - lambda_1)^{8m}",
  where z is the (1,-2)-homogeneous coordinate (the paper writes z = x^2 y in the parallel (9,24)
  argument, tex line 780; for direction (1,-2), x^2 y is the degree-0 monomial, consistent).
  Apply the morphism phi(x) = x, phi(y) = y + lambda_1 x^{-2}; then Pred_{phi(P)}(1,0) in {(1,-3),(2,-7)}.
  If Pred_{phi(P)}(1,0) = (2,-7), the lower side of phi(P) has only the corners m(-2,0), (0,0), m(28,8).
- If Pred_{phi(P)}(1,0) = (1,-3), there are one or two different linear factors in l_{1,-3}(phi(P)).
  One factor: apply x -> x, y -> y + lambda x^{-3} for adequate lambda; the lower side of P has only the
  corners m(-3,0), (0,0), m(28,8).
  Two factors: l_{1,-3}(P) = lambda x^{4m} (x^3 y - alpha_1)^{4m} (x^3 y - alpha_2)^{4m}. Apply
  x -> x, y -> y + alpha_1 x^{-3}; the lower side of P has only the corners m(-3,0), (0,0), m(16,4), m(28,8).

Resulting three possibilities for the (common, rescaled) Newton polygon:
  a) (m,n) times {(-2,0), (0,0), (28,8), (0,1)},
  b) (m,n) times {(-3,0), (0,0), (28,8), (0,1)},
  c) (m,n) times {(-3,0), (0,0), (16,4), (28,8), (0,1)}.
(The figures on p.11 are captioned "(1/2) N(P) = (1/3) N(Q) in the cases a) and b)" and "... in the
case c)", i.e. at this stage P carries factor 2 and Q factor 3.)

Step 3 (cut the top edge). [VERIFIED, tex line 1132] "In all the three cases the edge {(28,8),(1,0)}
must be of the form y (x^4 y - alpha)^7, corresponding to its form before the transformations. Apply
then the automorphism phi_3 given by phi_3(x) = x, phi_3(y) = y + alpha x^{-4}, reducing the edge
{(28,8),(0,1)} to {(28,8),(24,7)}."
(Note the printed sentence names the edge first as {(28,8),(1,0)} and then reduces {(28,8),(0,1)};
this apparent mismatch is in the source. The reduced edge is {(28,8),(24,7)} in all branches.)

Step 4 (opposite vertex analysis and [1, Prop 8.2]). [VERIFIED, tex lines 1132-1136]
"As in Proposition 4.1, one can analyze the possibilities for the opposite vertex (a,b) in the other
edge containing (24,7) and obtain that Succ_P(-1,4) = Succ_Q(-1,4) = (-2,7). We can apply
[1, Proposition 8.2] and get the existence of k in N with
  (k+1) b < a   and   {en_{rho,sigma}(P), en_{rho,sigma}(Q)} = {(-k,0), (k+1,1)},
where (a,b) is one of {(24,7), (17,5), (10,3), (3,1)} and (rho,sigma) is the direction corresponding to
the edge in question, obtaining k in {1,2}. The case k = 2 is impossible, as the edges of P and Q would
have no way of being parallel. In the case k = 1, we can set
(en_{rho,sigma}(Q), en_{rho,sigma}(P)) = ((2,1), (-1,0))."

Intermediate polygons [VERIFIED, tex lines 1137-1186]:
cases a), b): N(P) = {(-1,0), (0,0), 2(28,8), 2(24,7)}, N(Q) = {(2,1), (0,0), 3(28,8), 3(24,7)};
case c):      N(P) = {(-1,0), (0,0), 2(16,4), 2(28,8), 2(24,7)},
              N(Q) = {(2,1), (0,0), 3(16,4), 3(28,8), 3(24,7)}.

Step 5 (final inversion). [VERIFIED, tex line 1229] "Apply the the morphism phi with phi(x) = x^{-1}
and phi(y) = x^4 y. As in Proposition 4.1, this is not an automorphism and the chain rule gives
[phi(P), phi(Q)] = -[P,Q] x^2." A straightforward computation gives, in cases a) and b),
N(P) = {(0,0),(1,0),(8,14),(8,16)}, N(Q) = {(0,0),(2,1),(12,21),(12,24)} (case (2) of the statement),
and in case c) N(P) = {(0,0),(1,0),(8,14),(8,16),(0,8)}, N(Q) = {(0,0),(2,1),(12,21),(12,24),(0,12)}
(case (1) of the statement). QED.

[DERIVED, sanity check] In the reduced polygons deg_x P = 8 = 2*4, deg_x Q = 12 = 3*4, and the right
edges are P: (8,14)-(8,16), Q: (12,21)-(12,24); i.e. l_{1,0}(P) = x^8 y^14 (quadratic in y),
l_{1,0}(Q) = x^12 y^21 (cubic in y), compatible with l_{1,0}(P) = R^2, l_{1,0}(Q) = R^3 for
R = x^4 y^7 (a_0 + a_1 y). This is the exact structural analog of the (9,27) case (R = x^3 y^8(a_0+a_1 y)),
with the crucial difference [P,Q] = x^2 instead of x + g(y). The paper does not print this analog.

## 4. "The system of polynomial equations they could not solve"

### 4.1 What the paper actually says about the open case's system

[VERIFIED] The ONLY statement in [GGHV22] is the intro sentence (p.2): "For the other case with
(deg(P), deg(Q)) = (72,108) we couldn't solve the corresponding system of polynomial equations, thus
it is left open." Plus the compute remark: "With enough computing power we would be able to raise it up
from 108 to 125, since there is only one case left."

The paper NEVER writes down the (8,28) system. It has no theorem, no proposition, no equation display,
and no size estimate for that system. Sections 5 and 6 construct the systems only for (9,24)/(9,27)
and for (7,21). Any concrete (8,28) system must therefore be REBUILT by following the section-5/6
template starting from Proposition 4.3. Everything in 4.2-4.3 below is the verified template; 4.4 is
our derived sketch of the open-case analog, flagged as such.

### 4.2 The template: section 5 system for (9,24)/(9,27), transcribed in full (pp.14-19)

Theorem 5.1 [VERIFIED verbatim]: There exist no pair of polynomials P, Q in K[x,y] such that
 (1) [P,Q] = x + g(y) for some g(y) in K[y],
 (2) en_{3,-1}(P) = st_{1,0}(P) = 2(3,8) = (6,16) and st_{-1,1}(P) = en_{1,0}(P) = 2(3,9) = (6,18),
 (3) en_{3,-1}(Q) = st_{1,0}(Q) = 3(3,8) = (9,24) and st_{-1,1}(Q) = en_{1,0}(Q) = 3(3,9) = (9,27).

Setup [VERIFIED]: By [1, Propositions 1.13 and 2.1] there exists a (1,0)-homogeneous R with
l_{1,0}(P) = R^2 and l_{1,0}(Q) = R^3. Necessarily R = x^3 C_3 with C_3 = y^8 (a_0 + a_1 y),
a_0, a_1 in K^x; by a linear change of variables normalize C_3 = y^8 (y + 1).

Proposition 5.2 [VERIFIED]: There exist C, F in K[y, C_3^{-1}]((x^{-1})) with v_{1,0}(F) = -4 and
alpha_2, alpha_1, alpha_0, alpha_{-1} in K such that
 (1) P = C^2 and Q = C^3 + alpha_2 C^2 + alpha_1 C + alpha_0 + alpha_{-1} C^{-1} + F,
 (2) l_{1,0}(C) = x^3 C_3,
 (3) v_{-1,1}(C) = v_{-1,1}(3,9) = 6 (embedding K[y,C_3^{-1}] in K((y^{-1}))),
 (4) v_{3,-1}(C) = v_{3,-1}(3,8) = 1 (embedding K[y,C_3^{-1}] in K((y))).
Construction: C = x^3 C_3 + x^2 C_2 + x C_1 + C_0 + x^{-1} C_{-1} + ..., C_k in K[y, C_3^{-1}],
with the recursion (equation (5.2) of the paper)
  C_{3-k} := (1/(2 C_3)) ( P_{6-k} + sum_{j=1}^{k-1} C_{3-j} C_{3-k+j} )    [sign as printed: the tex
  shows C_{3-k} := -(1/(2C_3))(P_{6-k} + sum ...) at line 798 of the extracted text with a leading minus;
  the displayed proof derives 2 C_3 C_{3-k} = -P_{6-k} - sum, so C_{3-k} = -(1/(2C_3))(P_{6-k}+sum) ...
  note this fixes only conventions, both valuation bounds below are what matters],
and the two valuation bounds propagate: v_{-1,1}(C_{3-j} x^{3-j}) <= 6 and v_{3,-1}(C_{3-j} x^{3-j}) <= 1
for all j (using v_{-1,1}(C_3^{-1}) = -9 and v_{3,-1}(C_3^{-1}) = 8). The tail F satisfies
v_{1,0}(F) + v_{1,0}(P) - v_{1,0}(1,1) = v_{1,0}([F,P]), forcing v_{1,0}(F) = -4 (uses
v_{1,0}([Q - C^3, P]) = v_{1,0}(x) = 1).

Remark 5.3 [VERIFIED]: replacing Q by Q~ := Q - alpha_2 P - alpha_0 and P by P~ := P + (2/3) alpha_1
one may and does assume alpha_2 = alpha_1 = alpha_0 = 0, leaving (equation (5.6))

y(y+1) divides f, and deg(f) = 6. Proof machinery: from

## 5. The per-case algorithmic pipeline (for independent machine replication)

Assembled from the verified proofs; citations per step.

Phase A: case generation (from [GGHV17], see section 7 below).
 A1. Enumerate admissible complete chains (C_0,...,C_j, A_{j+1}) with v_{1,1}(A_0) <= bound
     ([GGHV17] Algorithm 8/"GetCompleteChains" pipeline, sections 2 and 5 there).
 A2. For each final corner A_{j+1} = (a/l, b), get all (m,n) via the Diophantine condition
     (m+n) b k - n(bl - a) = k (equations (3.17)/(3.18) of [GGHV17], Prop 3.2 there), packaged as
     arithmetic families (m_{k0} + j Delta_k^(1), n_{k0} + j Delta_k^(2)) (Algorithm 9,
     "GetmnFamilies"). Keep max{m,n} v_{1,1}(A_0) below the degree bound.

Phase B: polygon reduction ([GGHV22] section 4, "automorphisms reminiscent of the procedure in
section 8 of the ArXiv version of [1] (arXiv:1401.1784)", quote p.5).
 B1. Swap x <-> y (phi_1).
 B2. Identify forced edge leading forms via [1, Corollary 7.4]: for the direction of edge A_0-A_1,
     en(F) = (p/q)(a,b) forces l-form = (monomial)*(power of a single binomial)^q-structure;
     e.g. (8,28): en_{-1,4}(F) = (21,6) = (3/4)(28,8) gives q = 4 and
     l_{rho,sigma}(P) = lambda R^{4m}, en(R) = (7,2).
 B3. Constrain predecessor directions: [6, Proposition 2.5] and/or Algorithm 1
     "PossibleStartingPoints" ([GGHV22] p.8), transcribed exactly from the source:

       Procedure PossibleStartingPoints(M)
       Input:  a corner (a/l, b) in (1/l)N x N
       Output: a list of points (c/l, d) such that [R,G] = lambda R^i (eq. (4.1)) is satisfiable
       for d = 0 to b-1:
         for c = floor(d*a/b) + 1 to l*d + a - b*l - 1:
           N1 = gcd(a - c, b - d)
           N2 = gcd(c, d)
           (rho, sigma) = dir((a/l, b) - (c/l, d))
           s = (rho*a + sigma*l*b) / gcd(rho*a + sigma*l*b, l*(rho + sigma))
           if (s | N2 and d > 0) or s <= N1:
             append (c/l, d)
       return the list

     (Used with l = 1, a = 8, b = 3 in Prop 4.2. Setting context: [2, Proposition 3.12], where R is
     (rho,sigma)-homogeneous with [R,G] = lambda R^i.)
 B4. Kill candidate branches with [12, Proposition 10.2.6] (deg_x(P(x,0)) <= 0 impossible for
     P in kK[x,y]) and [2, Proposition 3.12](2) (forced factor multiplicities).
 B5. Cut edges by elementary L^(1)-automorphisms y -> y + lambda x^{-k} at each forced
     single-binomial-power edge (the lambda is the root of the binomial).
 B6. Opposite-vertex arithmetic: for the new edge through the cut vertex, candidate opposite vertices

 B7. Final inversion psi: x -> x^{-1}, y -> x^c y (c = 3 for (9,x), c = 4 for (8,28)); chain rule
